The 7 Key Metrics That Matter Most to Early Stage SaaS Companies

What metrics matter most for early stage SaaS companies? Annual recurring revenue (ARR) growth rate, Net Dollar Retention (NDR) Rate, Churn rate, Burn Multiple, Net Promoter Score (NPS), Burn rate, and Runway.

How to define, calculate, benchmark, and understand the importance of SaaS startup metrics.

As a software-as-a-service (SaaS) entrepreneur, and angel investor who writes checks into a lot of SaaS startups, I get this question all the time:

What metrics matter most for early stage SaaS companies?

While the answer to this question can be subjective and specific to the given business, there are some consistent key performance indicators to keep top of mind. In my opinion, these are the top seven:

  1. Annual recurring revenue (ARR) growth rate
  2. Net Dollar Retention (NDR) Rate
  3. Churn rate
  4. Burn Multiple
  5. Net Promoter Score (NPS)
  6. Burn rate
  7. Runway

In this essay, we'll review the definition, calculation, benchmarks, and importance of each of these metrics; and conclude with the key to driving metrics 1–5 in the aforementioned list.

1. Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) Growth Rate

Definition: Annual recurring revenue (ARR) growth rate is the growth rate of recurring revenue (from SaaS licenses) on an annual basis.

Calculation: When calculating ARR growth rate, you start by determining ARR. It is important to take new subscriptions, existing subscriptions, churned revenue, and upgrades and downgrades into account. Here is how to arrive at your ARR number prior to calculating the growth rate:

``` ARR Calculation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── (Total revenue from new subscriptions in a period) + (Recurring revenue from existing subscriptions at the beginning of the period) − (Churned revenue from existing subscriptions during the period) + (Upgrades or downgrades to existing subscriptions during the period) ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) ```

Once you have your ARR number, ARR growth rate is calculated by determining the difference between ARR at the end of a given time period and the beginning of that same period, then dividing by the ARR at the beginning of that period. It is expressed as a percentage.

For example: a company starts the year at $1M ARR and ends the year at $2M ARR. $2M − $1M = $1M difference. Divide $1M by $1M (the starting ARR) = 1, or 100% ARR growth rate.

``` ARR Growth Rate Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── (End of Period ARR − Start of Period ARR) ÷ Start of Period ARR × 100 ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = ARR Growth Rate (%) ```

Benchmark: SaaS ARR benchmarks can fluctuate with the market, but the 75th percentile of SaaS startups have the following corresponding ARR and ARR growth rates:

| ARR | ARR Growth Rate | |----------|----------------| | $1M | 493% | | $5M | 210% | | $10M | 157% | | $20M | 123% | | $50M | 93% | | $100M | 54% |

This roughly corresponds to Seed through Series E/pre-IPO stages.

Importance: Startups are defined by their ability to grow exponentially, with the goal of disrupting their given market. SaaS companies are almost always judged by their revenue from the very start.

2. Net Dollar Retention (NDR) Rate

Definition: Net dollar retention rate is a percentage reflecting how annual recurring revenue has grown or shrunk within a defined time period for a given cohort of customers, taking upgrades, downgrades, and churn into account. Think of it this way:

For a cohort of customers you had 12 months ago, how much are they paying you 12 months later?

Calculation: NDR is calculated by taking your starting ARR for a cohort, adding upgrades, subtracting downgrades and churn, then dividing by the original ARR of that cohort.

``` Net Dollar Retention (NDR) Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Starting MRR + Upgrade MRR − Downgrade MRR − Churned MRR ÷ Starting MRR × 100 ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Net Dollar Retention Rate (%) ```

Benchmark: Top-performing SaaS companies have 120%+ NDR, with the median at 106%. Below 100% merits further investigation into the product or business model.

Importance: NDR shows companies (and their investors) how much growth they're achieving regardless of whether they acquire any new customers. Ideally a SaaS company has effective ways of retaining customers and expanding revenue by getting them to upgrade for more features and access.

3. Churn Rate

Definition: SaaS churn is the percentage rate at which SaaS customers cancel their recurring revenue subscriptions on a monthly or annual basis. Put simply, it's the rate at which you lose customers and customer revenue.

There are two kinds of churn:

  • Revenue churn — how much revenue is lost by customers not renewing or canceling during a given period.
  • Logo churn — how many customers cancel or fail to renew their subscriptions during a given period.

Calculation: To determine revenue churn, take your MRR at the beginning of the month — minus any upgrades or additional revenue from existing customers — and divide by the MRR you lost that month. Do not include new sales in the month, as you are looking for how much total revenue you lost.

``` Revenue Churn Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── (MRR Start of Month − MRR End of Month) − Upgrade MRR During Month ÷ Starting MRR ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Revenue Churn Rate (%) ```

``` Logo Churn Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── (Customers Start of Month − Customers End of Month) ÷ Customers Start of Month ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Logo Churn Rate (%) ```

Benchmark: Churn rate depends heavily on the business model and customer base. As a general rule of thumb, a good annual churn rate for SaaS is between 5% and 7% annually. A SaaS company selling to mid-market enterprise clients may be healthy at 5%–7% annual churn, whereas one selling to prosumers may be healthy at 5%–7% per month.

Importance: Churn rate is indicative of how "sticky" a product is, and lost customers equal lost revenue. If churn is higher than expected, look to your product analytics for events that may be triggering churn, and talk to your customers about why they are leaving.

4. Burn Multiple

Definition: Burn Multiple is a measure of capital efficiency — it measures how much capital a startup is burning in order to generate each incremental dollar of ARR. The higher the Burn Multiple, the more capital is being burned to achieve growth. The lower, the more efficient the growth is.

Calculation: Divide net burn by net new ARR.

For example: a startup burned $2M last quarter and added $1M in ARR. The Burn Multiple is 2x.

``` Burn Multiple Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Burn Multiple ```

Benchmark:

| Burn Multiple | Efficiency Rating | |---------------|---------------------| | < 1x | Exceptional | | 1x – 1.5x | Great | | 1.5x – 2x | Okay | | 2x – 3x | Inefficient | | > 3x | Very Inefficient |

Importance: Burn Multiple is an all-encompassing metric — any serious capital efficiency problem in the business will eventually impact it.

  • < 1x: Pour cash into thriving growth channels and test new ones.
  • 1x – 2x: Continue investing in sales and marketing, but optimize without significantly increasing spend.
  • > 2x: Something is wrong. Investigate before investing more into sales and marketing — it could be a flaw in your business model, your product experience, or a fundamental product market fit issue.

5. Net Promoter Score (NPS)

Definition: Net Promoter Score (NPS) is a survey and calculation that measures customer experience and satisfaction, and predicts business growth.

Calculation: NPS asks one simple question:

How likely is it that you would recommend [Startup X] to a friend or colleague?

Respondents rate from 0 (not at all likely) to 10 (extremely likely), and fall into one of three categories:

  • Promoters — score of 9 or 10
  • Passives — score of 7 or 8
  • Detractors — score of 0 to 6

``` NPS Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── % of Promoters − % of Detractors ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = NPS Score (range: −100 to +100) ```

Scores closer to −100 indicate more detractors overall. Scores closer to +100 indicate more promoters overall.

Benchmark: A good NPS score for SaaS companies hovers around 28, though it's dependent on the relevant competitive landscape.

Importance: NPS measures customer satisfaction and loyalty, and how likely customers are to refer a company's products and services to others.

6. Burn Rate

Definition: Burn rate is the rate at which a company is spending its capital to finance overhead before generating positive cash flow from operations. It is a measure of negative cash flow, usually quoted in cash spent per month.

Calculation:

``` Burn Rate Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Cash Received − Cash Spent ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Monthly Burn Rate ```

Benchmark: A good burn rate is around one-eighteenth of your available cash, targeting at least 18 months of runway. If you have $1,000,000 in available cash, a burn rate close to $55,000/month would be good.

Importance: When burn rate is high, there's a risk of running out of cash before the next fundraise or revenue growth breakeven point. When a startup has really strong growth and is making a move to dominate the market, it can make sense to raise capital and increase burn rate. Absent that, high burn is often indicative of poor capital efficiency and a precursor to short runway.

7. Runway

Definition: Runway is the amount of time the company has before it runs out of money, usually quoted in months. For example: a company with $1M in the bank that spends $100K/month and brings in no revenue has a burn rate of $100K and runway of 10 months.

Calculation: Runway is a "point in time" calculation — it changes with every hire or revenue movement. It's also important to remember that expenses and burn aren't the same thing: you can "spend" $100K in a month without burning anything so long as you're bringing in $100K or more that month.

``` Runway Formula ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Cash in the Bank ÷ Monthly Burn Rate ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ = Runway (in months) ```

Benchmark: I recommend founders keep at least 18 months of runway. Conventional wisdom suggests 12 months is a lower bound, but if you're aiming for 12, you may quickly find yourself at 9 — and that's the danger zone. For the current fundraising environment, aim for 24+ months of runway.

Importance: Runway isn't just about the time before the company runs out of money — it informs how much leverage the company will have in fundraising negotiations. Low runway can be a red flag for good investors, and can attract investors who want to leverage the dire state of the company for better deal terms. That said, if your company is almost out of money: it's better to take venture capital on subpar terms than not raise at all, because once you run out of runway — that's a wrap on your startup.

The Key Metrics That Move the Key Metrics

At the start of this essay, I mentioned I would provide insight into what drives ARR growth rate, NDR, Churn, and NPS — and a lot of that has to do with product engagement.

Three of my favorite questions to ask prospective angel investments:

  • How do clients associate value with your product?
  • What is their value-associated in-product behavior and how do you measure it?
  • What do those metrics look like?

Product metrics that show a high level of engagement around value-associated behaviors (e.g. publishing edits to a landing page, generating an analytics report, reviewing a call recording) are extremely telling — they are often a precursor to paid plan conversion, increased NDR (via upgrades), reduced churn, and higher NPS numbers.

Watch these seven numbers carefully, but make sure to keep focus and effort on building the best product for your customers.
